The gut-skin axis

Foundational

Recent reviews in Life (2024) and Current Nutrition Reports (2025) converge on one point: a well-balanced, plant-based pattern feeds the gut microbiome, which in turn lowers systemic inflammation driving acne, psoriasis, atopic dermatitis and hidradenitis suppurativa.

The mechanism is not mystical. Fibre from plants ferments into short-chain fatty acids (butyrate especially), which strengthen the gut barrier, calm immune signalling and reduce the low-grade inflammation that shows up on your face.

Target: 30+ different plant species per week (Tim Spector, ZOE data). Herbs, spices, nuts, seeds and beans all count. It is diversity, not just volume, that shifts the microbiome.

Antioxidants and polyphenols

Skin aging

A 2020 review in The Journal of Clinical and Aesthetic Dermatology looked at diet and telomere length and concluded that whole-food, plant-based patterns high in antioxidants and low in "gerontotoxins" (advanced glycation end-products from fried, charred, ultra-processed food) protect skin from accelerated aging.

Lycopene

Reduces UV-induced erythema.

Cooked tomatoes, watermelon, pink grapefruit, guava.

Mechanism
A carotenoid that quenches singlet oxygen (a reactive form generated by UV) more efficiently than any other dietary antioxidant. It embeds in skin cell membranes and acts as an internal sunscreen booster (not a replacement for SPF).

Evidence
A 2011 UK trial (Rhodes et al.) had participants eat 55g tomato paste daily for 12 weeks. Result: 33% less UV-induced erythema and higher procollagen levels than controls.

How to eat it
Cooking with fat (olive oil) increases bioavailability roughly 3-4x by converting trans- to cis-lycopene. A daily spoon of good tomato passata cooked with olive oil is more useful than raw tomatoes.

Beta-carotene

Precursor to vitamin A. Skin cell turnover.

Sweet potato, carrot, red pepper, mango, kale.

Mechanism
Converts to retinol in the gut, which regulates keratinocyte differentiation (how quickly skin cells mature and shed). Adequate vitamin A means smoother texture and normal sebum flow. Deficiency shows as dry, rough, "goose-flesh" skin (phrynoderma).

Evidence
Serum carotenoid levels correlate with skin yellow-tone attractiveness ratings (Whitehead et al., 2012). It also modestly protects against sunburn over 10+ weeks of supplementation.

How to eat it
Fat-soluble: always with oil. A roasted sweet potato with olive oil gives more usable carotene than a raw carrot stick. Very high intake tints skin orange (carotenaemia) — harmless, and reversible in a week.

Polyphenols (EGCG)

Anti-inflammatory, photoprotective.

Green tea, matcha, cocoa, berries, extra virgin olive oil.

Mechanism
EGCG (epigallocatechin-3-gallate) inhibits MMP-1, the enzyme UV light activates to degrade collagen. It also blocks NF-kB inflammatory signalling and scavenges reactive oxygen species directly.

Evidence
A 12-week study (Heinrich et al., 2011) with 1402 mg green tea polyphenols daily showed reduced UV-induced erythema, improved skin elasticity and increased blood flow to the dermis.

How to drink it
2-3 cups of green tea daily, brewed 2-3 minutes at 80°C (boiling water destroys EGCG). Matcha delivers 3x more per gram. Do not drink with meals if you're iron-conscious: tannins block non-heme iron absorption.

Vitamin C

Cofactor for collagen synthesis.

Peppers, kiwi, citrus, parsley, broccoli. Eat with iron-rich foods.

Mechanism
Required to hydroxylate proline and lysine residues on procollagen. Without it, collagen cannot cross-link properly and skin loses tensile strength (extreme case: scurvy). Also regenerates oxidised vitamin E and inhibits tyrosinase (the pigment enzyme), so it lightly brightens tone.

Evidence
Higher dietary vitamin C intake correlates with fewer wrinkles and less senile dryness in observational studies (Cosgrove et al., 2007, from the US NHANES cohort).

How to eat it
Water-soluble and heat-sensitive: raw or lightly cooked. A red pepper has 3x the vitamin C of an orange. Pair with plant iron sources (lentils, spinach) to boost non-heme iron absorption up to 4x.

Vitamin E

Lipid-phase antioxidant. Works synergistically with C.

Sunflower seeds, almonds, hazelnuts, avocado, olive oil.

Mechanism
Alpha-tocopherol embeds in cell membranes and stops lipid peroxidation, the chain reaction where free radicals destroy membrane fats. Vitamin C then regenerates spent vitamin E, which is why they are always studied together.

Evidence
Combined oral C + E supplementation reduces the minimal erythema dose response to UV, meaning skin burns less easily. Neither works nearly as well alone.

How to eat it
A small handful (30g) of sunflower seeds delivers roughly the daily requirement (15 mg). Almonds, hazelnuts and avocado are the runners-up. No supplement needed for a varied diet.

Sulforaphane

Activates Nrf2 detox pathway. Anti-aging signal.

Broccoli sprouts (highest), broccoli, rocket, cabbage.

Mechanism
The strongest known natural activator of Nrf2, a transcription factor that upregulates dozens of endogenous antioxidant and detoxification enzymes (glutathione, NQO1, HO-1). Effect lasts days after eating, unlike direct antioxidants which are used up on contact.

Evidence
Talalay et al. (Johns Hopkins) showed topical and oral broccoli sprout extract reduced UV-induced erythema by up to 37%. Repeated in multiple human trials since.

How to eat it
3-day broccoli sprouts contain 20-50x more sulforaphane than mature broccoli. Chop or chew thoroughly (the enzyme myrosinase must contact the precursor). If cooking broccoli, brief steaming (3-4 min) preserves it; boiling destroys it.

Omega-3s: the barrier lipid

Skin barrier + shine

EPA and DHA are the anti-inflammatory omega-3s that keep the skin barrier resilient and hair shafts less brittle. On a plant diet you get ALA (from flax, chia, walnuts, hemp), but conversion to EPA/DHA is inefficient (roughly 5-10% for EPA, under 1% for DHA).

The clean fix: algae oil. It is where fish get their omega-3 in the first place. Aim for around 250-500 mg combined EPA + DHA daily. Brands like Testa, Norsan Vegan and Nordic Naturals Algae do this well.

Note for you specifically: high-histamine fish (mackerel, sardines, tuna) would be off the table anyway. Algae oil is histamine-friendly.

Glycemic load and dairy

Acne + oiliness

The strongest dietary link in acne research is not chocolate. It is high-glycemic-load diets and, in some cohorts, skim milk. Both spike insulin and IGF-1, which drives sebum production and follicular keratinisation.

Swapwhite bread → sourdough, oats, quinoa
Swapsugary drinks → water, unsweetened tea
Swapskim dairy → oat, soy, almond (unsweetened)
Keepwhole fruit (fibre buffers the sugar)

Nutrients hair actually needs

Density + growth

Hair follicles are metabolically demanding. Deficiency in any of the following can push follicles into the resting phase and cause diffuse shedding. Cleveland Clinic and Healthline reviews (2025-2026) consistently flag the same shortlist.

Iron (ferritin 40-70 ng/mL)

Single most common cause of diffuse hair loss.

Lentils, tofu, tempeh, pumpkin seeds, spinach, dark chocolate. Take with vitamin C.

Mechanism
Iron is required by ribonucleotide reductase, the enzyme that lets rapidly-dividing cells (like hair matrix cells) synthesise DNA. When iron drops, the body prioritises red blood cells and other essentials; follicles get cut off first and shift into the telogen (resting) phase 2-3 months later.

Evidence
Multiple studies (Trost, Bergfeld et al., 2006) show low ferritin correlates with telogen effluvium and female-pattern hair loss. Threshold for hair symptoms is typically ferritin under 40 ng/mL, though full labs may report "normal" down to 15.

How to eat it
Non-heme (plant) iron absorbs at 2-20% vs heme (animal) at 15-35%. Boost absorption: pair with vitamin C (lentils + pepper), avoid tea/coffee within 1 hour of meals (tannins block it), and use a cast iron pan for acidic cooking.

Zinc (11-15 mg daily)

Cofactor for keratin synthesis.

Pumpkin seeds, cashews, chickpeas, oats, hemp seeds.

Mechanism
Required for protein synthesis and cell division in the hair matrix, plus stabilising the hair shaft's keratin proteins. Also modulates hedgehog signalling, which regulates the anagen (growth) phase of the follicle cycle.

Evidence
Low serum zinc is documented in alopecia areata, telogen effluvium and male-pattern baldness. Supplementation only helps if deficient.

How to eat it
Pumpkin seeds are the plant king (10 mg per 100g). Phytates in unprocessed grains and legumes block absorption by ~50%, so soaking, sprouting, fermenting (sourdough) or lightly toasting helps significantly.

Protein (1.2-1.6 g per kg body weight)

Hair is 95% keratin. Undereating protein thins hair fast.

Tofu, tempeh, seitan, edamame, lentils, quinoa, soy yoghurt.

Mechanism
Hair matrix cells need a constant supply of sulphur-containing amino acids (cysteine and methionine) to build keratin's disulphide bridges — the bonds that make hair strong. Prolonged low protein intake pushes follicles into telogen within 2-3 months.

Evidence
Documented in crash-dieters, gastric-bypass patients and low-calorie vegetarians. Hair shedding usually reverses 3-6 months after protein intake normalises.

How to eat it
For a 75 kg adult, that's 90-120 g protein/day. Rough plant portions: 100g tofu (17g), 100g tempeh (19g), 100g cooked lentils (9g), 30g hemp seeds (10g), 1 cup soy milk (7g). Spread it across 3-4 meals.

Biotin (B7)

Only helps if you are deficient. Rare on a varied diet.

Sunflower seeds, almonds, sweet potato, mushrooms.

Mechanism
A cofactor for carboxylase enzymes involved in fat and amino acid metabolism, including keratin production. True deficiency causes hair loss, brittle nails and rash — but it's genuinely rare because gut bacteria produce it.

Evidence
The hair-growth supplement industry runs on biotin marketing. Reviews consistently find no benefit in non-deficient people. Worse: mega-doses (over 5,000 mcg) interfere with thyroid and cardiac blood tests.

How to eat it
Skip the gummies. A varied plant diet with sunflower seeds, almonds, oats, sweet potato and mushrooms covers the 30 mcg daily requirement several times over.

Vitamin D (above 30 ng/mL)

Regulates hair follicle cycling. Widely deficient even in Spain.

Sunlight plus a D3 supplement (lichen-derived if vegan).

Mechanism
The vitamin D receptor (VDR) is expressed in the hair follicle and regulates entry into the anagen (active growth) phase. Mice lacking VDR develop alopecia. In humans, low vitamin D is linked to telogen effluvium and alopecia areata.

Evidence
Spanish adult population studies show 40-80% under 30 ng/mL depending on the region and season — the "Spanish paradox" is that people avoid midday sun and cover up, so the sunshine hours don't translate to serum levels.

How to top up
15-20 minutes of arms-and-face sun without SPF between 11:00-15:00 in summer. In winter (October-April), 1000-2000 IU D3 daily. Test 25(OH)D once a year; adjust dose accordingly.

Silica

Supports connective tissue around the follicle.

Oats, bananas, cucumber (with skin), horsetail tea.

Mechanism
Silica cross-links collagen and elastin fibres in the dermis and around the follicle, giving structural support. It also incorporates directly into the hair shaft, contributing to tensile strength.

Evidence
A 9-month randomised trial with choline-stabilised orthosilicic acid (Wickett et al., 2007) showed measurable improvements in hair tensile strength and thickness. Effect is real but modest.

How to eat it
Oats at breakfast is the easiest daily source. Horsetail (cola de caballo) tea, sold at any Spanish herbolario, gives a concentrated dose — 1 cup a day is enough.

Vegan and veggie blind spots

Honest list

A well-planned plant-based diet is protective for skin. A badly-planned one is worse than mixed eating because these nutrients quietly go missing and both skin and hair suffer:

Vitamin B12

Non-negotiable supplement on a vegan diet.

Cyanocobalamin 1000 mcg 2-3× per week, or 250 mcg daily.

Mechanism
Essential for DNA synthesis and methylation. Rapidly-dividing cells (bone marrow, gut lining, hair matrix) are affected first. Deficiency shows in skin as hyperpigmentation, glossitis (smooth swollen tongue), and diffuse hair thinning.

Evidence
Plants contain zero bioavailable B12. Nutritional yeast and some seaweeds are fortified or contain analogues, but reliable intake requires a supplement. Long-term deficiency causes irreversible nerve damage.

How to supplement
Cyanocobalamin is cheap, stable and effective. 1000 mcg twice a week, or 250 mcg daily. Higher doses if you're over 50 (absorption drops). Test serum B12 and MMA yearly.

Vitamin D3

Choose D3 from lichen, not D2. Better retention.

1000-2000 IU daily, more in winter.

Mechanism
D3 (cholecalciferol) is roughly twice as effective as D2 (ergocalciferol) at raising and maintaining serum 25(OH)D levels. Traditional D3 comes from lanolin (sheep's wool); vegan D3 is now available from lichen at the same potency.

Evidence
Meta-analyses (Tripkovic et al., 2012) consistently show D3 outperforms D2 for raising and sustaining serum levels, especially with less-frequent dosing.

How to supplement
Take with a fatty meal (fat-soluble). Brands like Viridian, Vivo Life and Terranova make lichen D3. Combine with K2 (MK-7) if supplementing above 2000 IU long-term for calcium routing.

Algae EPA/DHA

ALA conversion is not enough. Supplement directly.

250-500 mg combined per day.

Mechanism
ALA (flax, chia, walnut) has to be enzymatically converted to EPA and then DHA. Conversion is 5-10% for EPA and under 1% for DHA in most adults. Men convert less efficiently than women (oestrogen upregulates the enzyme).

Evidence
Algae oil raises serum EPA and DHA equivalently to fish oil at the same dose (Arterburn et al., 2008). It's also cleaner: no heavy metals, no PCBs, no fishy burp.

How to supplement
250-500 mg combined EPA + DHA daily, taken with a meal containing fat. Refrigerate after opening; oxidised omega-3 is worse than none.

Iodine

Thyroid drives hair growth. Vegans often under.

A pinch of iodised salt daily, or 150 mcg supplement.

Mechanism
Iodine builds thyroid hormone (T3, T4), which regulates the metabolic rate of every cell — including hair follicles. Hypothyroidism causes coarse, dry, brittle hair and diffuse loss, especially the outer eyebrows.

Evidence
Vegan diets in areas without fortified salt (Spain, much of Europe) show a high prevalence of iodine insufficiency. Kelp and other seaweeds are rich but wildly inconsistent in dose (some kombu is dangerously high).

How to eat it
A pinch of iodised salt daily is the simplest fix. Or a low-dose supplement (150 mcg, the RDA). Avoid random seaweed dosing unless the product states iodine content.

Iron (ferritin, tested yearly)

Non-heme iron absorbs less efficiently.

Legumes plus vitamin C. Avoid tea/coffee with meals.

Mechanism
Same follicle mechanism as in section 5, but worth flagging again here: vegetarians and vegans need roughly 1.8x the RDA to compensate for lower absorption. Test ferritin, not just haemoglobin — you can be functionally iron-deficient for hair before you're anaemic.

Evidence
The World Health Organisation adjusts iron RDA upward for plant-based eaters explicitly for this reason.

How to eat it
Lentils, tofu, tempeh, pumpkin seeds, quinoa — always with a vitamin C source in the same meal (lemon, pepper, tomato, kiwi). Cook in cast iron. Space tea and coffee at least 1 hour from iron-rich meals.

Zinc (phytate-adjusted)

Phytates in grains and legumes reduce absorption. Soak, sprout, ferment.

Sourdough, sprouted lentils, soaked oats.

Mechanism
Phytic acid, present in the outer layers of grains and legumes, binds zinc (and iron) in the gut and stops it from being absorbed. Traditional processing methods (soaking overnight, sprouting, sourdough fermentation) release phytase enzymes that break phytates down.

Evidence
Sourdough sprouting increases mineral absorption up to 3x compared to yeasted bread. Soaking oats overnight has a similar effect.

How to eat it
Overnight-soaked oats for breakfast, sourdough over regular bread, and pumpkin seeds as your daily zinc anchor (30 g covers you). Vegan zinc RDA is roughly 50% higher than the standard, so ~14-18 mg/day.

The daily plate

Practical

Not a rigid plan. A checklist to loosely hit over a normal day.

  • A big handful of leafy greens (spinach, rocket, kale)
  • Two portions of colourful veg (peppers, tomatoes, carrots, beetroot)
  • Two whole fruits (berries prioritised for polyphenols)
  • One serving of legumes (lentils, chickpeas, tofu, tempeh, edamame)
  • A small handful of nuts and seeds (walnuts, flax, chia, pumpkin)
  • Extra virgin olive oil, raw, over finished food
  • 2 litres of water. Herbal tea counts. Alcohol works against everything above.
  • Fermented food if tolerated (sauerkraut, kimchi, live yoghurt)
Histamine note (for you specifically): spinach, tomatoes, fermented foods and aged soy products are high-histamine. Rotate them, and if flares happen, swap in rocket, cucumber, courgette, fresh chickpeas and hemp seeds.

Hair and scalp

For scalp and hair: rosemary oil massage (a 2015 trial found it comparable to 2% minoxidil at 6 months) three times a week, left on for 30 minutes before washing. Avoid over-washing: twice weekly with a gentle shampoo is enough.

Sleep, stress, sunlight

The multipliers

Nutrition and skincare underperform without these three.

Sleep

Skin regeneration and collagen synthesis peak between 22:00 and 02:00. Chronic sleep deprivation raises cortisol, which degrades collagen and worsens acne. Non-negotiable: 7-8 hours, dark room, cool temperature.

Stress

Cortisol drives sebum, IGF-1 and inflammation. Whatever downregulates yours (walking, breathwork, cold exposure, lifting) is a legitimate skincare intervention.

Sunlight

Ten to twenty minutes of morning sun without sunscreen sets your circadian rhythm and helps vitamin D. Everything after that needs SPF. UV is the number one driver of visible aging, well ahead of anything on your plate.
