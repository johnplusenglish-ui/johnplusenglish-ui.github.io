After Joe Hudson · On self-worth & attachment

The wisdom
of anger

It was never the emotion to be managed. Felt cleanly, anger is information — about what you love, and where a line was crossed. Learning to feel it is part of learning that your needs are allowed to matter.

Read on

Hudson's image: the anger is the same water. The story you wrap around it is the kink in the hose.

🔥

The Basics

What anger is, why it feels unsafe, and how to use it cleanly.

i.

🔥 Stop managing it. Feel it.

Hudson's core move is to treat anger not as something to suppress or to hurl at someone, but as an emotion to feel fully and cleanly. Stop resisting it and it stops running you. What comes through then isn't destruction — it's boundary.

When you're fully allowing yourself to feel that anger, it comes out like Gandhi or Martin Luther King — with boundary and clarity and dignity. It's the resistance that's uncomfortable.

Joe Hudson

The resistance is the kink: the story you wrap around the feeling. You always do this. If you loved me you would. I hate you. Those distortions are what turn a clean signal into something that wounds — the leak in the second hose above.

ii.

🫥 When anger feels unsafe to have

If your wiring leans anxious or insecure in attachment, anger is often the least safe thing to feel. Somewhere early it became dangerous — it risked the love you depended on. So it gets pushed down. But suppressed anger doesn't leave. It changes direction.

When anger isn't allowed outward, it turns inward. You start attacking yourself — self-judgments, harsh internal commentary, picking apart every mistake.

Joe Hudson · the signs of repressed anger

Here's the link straight back to self-worth: when you can't honour your anger, your boundaries stay unprotected and your needs go unmet — which quietly confirms the old belief that your needs don't count. Letting anger inform one clean boundary is how you prove to yourself that they do.

  • i
    Self-criticism — a relentless inner voice, rage redirected onto yourself.
  • ii
    Flatness — low-grade depression, the stuck and colourless feeling.
  • iii
    Passive-aggression — the sideways jab instead of the direct ask.
  • iv
    Anxiety — looping, overthinking; emotion wearing a rational disguise.
  • v
    Resentment — leaking onto people who didn't cause it.
Hudson's tell: to find the emotion you're avoiding, look at the one you keep accidentally creating. Suppress anger → you get passive-aggression. Avoid feeling like a failure → you don't try → you feel like a failure anyway.
iii.

⛏️ Mine it before you move

When anger arrives, don't act yet — read it. Anger always points to something you value. These are the three questions Hudson asks to get under the heat to the need beneath it.

1
What do I care about so deeply that my anger is fighting for it?
2
What boundary has been crossed?
3
What is the clarity my anger is seeking?
iv.

📖 Feel the heat, doubt the story

Hudson splits the two apart: the feeling of anger is clean information; the story it tells you is where the damage lives.

Your emotions control you when you believe the stories they tell. Anger says you've been disrespected — so you lash out and burn bridges.

Joe Hudson

The skill is to feel the anger fully in the body while holding the story lightly — watching yourself as an actor rather than vanishing into "they're wrong and they deserve this."

A caveat tuned for you: if you tend toward freeze or shutdown — common with anxious attachment — the pure observer stance can switch anger off entirely and leave you feeling nothing. For you it may help to lean into the story just enough that the anger can move and you can actually feel it, rather than intellectualising it away.
v.

🍃 You'll know by what's left

The proof that anger did its work isn't more fight. It's relief.

At the end of an anger movement you get clarity and determination. It's a visceral sense — like relief. "Aha, I know what to do. There's no more fight in me."

Joe Hudson

If you end up more wound up — more certain the other person is awful — that's the tell that you got lost in the story, or that anger wasn't even the right emotion and there's grief or fear underneath. Hudson notes the emotions tend to move in order: anger, then sadness, then fear, then the quieter things.

vi.

🔀 Same heat, two endings

When it does need voicing, the difference between a rupture and a boundary is ownership. One defends the story; the other protects the need.

The kink

"You always do this. You don't even care."

Clean

"I'm angry — because I needed this, and it didn't happen."

Same energy, opposite effect — on the relationship, and on your own self-respect.

vii.

🪤 Self-abandonment: the hidden cost

Hudson has a sharp name for what happens when you swallow anger to keep someone close: self-abandonment. You manage and appease the other person's feelings instead of having your own — and you leave yourself in order to not be left.

Self-abandonment in an attempt to prevent others from leaving sets you up for a hollow existence filled with resentment.

Joe Hudson · paraphrased

It's a cruel trade, because it backfires by Hudson's own "ironic" logic: the appeasing produces the very passive-aggression and isolation you feared, and the resentment leaks into the closeness you were trying to protect. For anxious or insecure attachment, naming your anger is the antidote to self-abandonment — it's the moment you choose to stay with yourself.

viii.

🛟 The core belief to overturn: anger can be safe

In Hudson's courses there's a striking exercise — people take turns voicing anger out loud (no targets, no hitting) while others simply witness it. Participants describe covering their faces, flinching, discovering how frightened they are of anger, their own and others'. And then something shifts.

The lesson for the mind-body system is simple: anger can be safe.

On Hudson's "anger movement" practice

If anger felt dangerous growing up — loud and frightening, or silently punished — your nervous system filed it under threat. The repair isn't insight, it's experience: feeling anger, even a flicker of it, and discovering that nothing breaks. You don't disintegrate. You don't get abandoned. The room is still standing. That lived proof is what loosens the old rule.

Mindset

The toolkit behind it — the state of mind that lets you feel anything.

ix.

🪟 The wider mindset: VIEW

Anger is one doorway into a larger shift Hudson teaches — a state of mind he calls VIEW. It's the posture that lets you feel anything, anger included, without being run by it. Four qualities, held at once:

V
Vulnerability

Actually feeling it, rather than defending against it.

I
Impartiality

Not trying to change the feeling or make it go away.

E
Empathy

Warmth toward yourself for having the feeling at all.

W
Wonder

Curiosity about what it is and what it's pointing to.

The everyday version: when anger rises, can you be vulnerable enough to feel it, impartial enough not to wrestle it, kind enough not to judge yourself for it, and curious enough to ask what it wants? That's "using anger well" in four moves.

🐢 Hudson's image for Wonder: look at the emotion "as if you're a kid finding a turtle for the first time." Not what's wrong with me for feeling this, but oh — what's this? That single shift, from verdict to curiosity, is most of the work.
x.

🪢 It's an undoing, not a doing

This is the heart of Hudson's mindset, and the part most likely to flip something for you. The work isn't to do something with anger — process it, fix it, manage it down. The effort is all in the resistance. Drop the resistance and the emotion moves on its own.

It's an undoing, not a doing. The doing is in the resistance to the emotion — that's what takes energy and effort. I have more energy if I experience an emotion unresisted.

Joe Hudson

His reasoning for why you'd welcome something as unwelcome as anger is blunt and, for someone doing self-worth work, oddly freeing:

You've spent your whole life trying to get rid of it, and it hasn't worked. That's the reason you welcome it — because each one of these emotions was a rejected part of you.

Joe Hudson

That reframes the whole project. Welcoming your anger isn't anger management — it's self-reclamation. The part of you that learned anger was unsafe is the part you're inviting back in. Hudson also notes resistance itself isn't the enemy: if you can't love the feeling, love the resistance to the feeling. Even the clenching is allowed.

Where you'll feel it: Hudson points out we don't only think with the brain — there's a "neural network" in the heart and the gut too. Anger often lives lower down, as a clenched gut you've stopped noticing. Part of the undoing is simply letting that unclench.
xi.

🚪 Why feeling one opens the rest

Emotions, in Hudson's model, travel together and in sequence — the "anger, sadness, fear" sandwich his students describe. Anger is frequently the bouncer at the door: feel it cleanly and what's underneath (often grief, or fear of being left) can finally surface. Refuse it and you lock the whole family out, including the good ones.

Joy is the matriarch of a family of emotions. She won't come into your house if her children are not welcome.

Joe Hudson

This is the quiet argument against numbing your anger to keep the peace: you can't selectively close down. The same door that keeps anger out keeps joy out. Letting yourself be angry well is, strangely, part of how you let yourself be glad.

Emotions

The bigger picture anger sits inside — Hudson's model of how feeling actually works.

xii.

🌊 Emotional fluidity

This is the master idea under everything else. Hudson's goal isn't to manage emotions or regulate them down — it's emotional fluidity: the capacity to feel any emotion fully, let it move, and not be ruled by it.

Emotional fluidity is allowing yourself to feel all the emotions — and not be ruled by them.

Joe Hudson

He draws a hard line between clarity and management. Management means tightening down the system to keep feelings small — and Hudson says that tightening is itself the source of much of our stress. Fluidity is the opposite motion: loosening, so the emotion can pass through like water rather than sitting stagnant. Defensiveness, he notes, is what makes emotions stagnate.

Why it's the root of the self-worth work: Hudson says you can't selectively numb. Suppress anger and you dampen joy, love, aliveness along with it. Reclaiming the full range — starting with the "negative" ones you were taught to hide — is how the good ones become available again.
xiii.

🪜 The order emotions move in

Hudson observes a reliable sequence when people start letting feeling flow. You usually can't leap straight to the good stuff — the doormen come first.

🔥Angerthe first gate — boundary, protest
↓
💧Sadnessthe grief the anger was guarding
↓
🌫️Fearwhat felt too vulnerable to admit
↓
🕊️Peace · joy · lovewhat's underneath, once the gates open

You can't just jump to the positive things and actually have a full sensation of them. It happens in that order.

Joe Hudson

This explains why "just think positive" rarely lands, and why anger matters so much: for many people it's the first accessible feeling, the one that unlocks the rest. (Hudson notes it varies — for some, sadness is more reachable than anger, and the order flips.) If you feel anger and then unexpected tears arrive, that's not a detour. That's the sequence working.

xiv.

🧠 Three brains: head, heart, gut

Hudson teaches that you don't think with your head alone — there are dense neural networks in the heart and the gut too, each with its own intelligence. Emotional work means consulting all three, not just the loud verbal one upstairs.

🧠Head

Stories, analysis, beliefs. Fast and verbal — and the one most prone to spinning a narrative over the feeling.

❤️Heart

The felt emotion itself — tenderness, hurt, warmth, grief. The seat of emotional fluidity.

🔥Gut

Instinct, power, anger, yes/no. Hudson says most of us clench here constantly without noticing.

A practice he offers: ask a question and let each one answer in turn — what does my head say? my heart? my gut? The gut, he jokes, "doesn't speak good English" — its answers come as sensation, heat, an animal sound, a clench or a release. Anger usually lives in the gut, which is why it can be invisible to a head-dominant person until they drop their attention lower.

🎯 Try this: Hudson suggests keeping ~10% of your attention inside your body at all times — even one inch below the navel during a conversation. It sounds trivial; it's not. It's how you catch the gut clenching in real time, which is how you catch anger before it becomes a story.

Tools

Hudson's actual practices — the moves you can use when anger (or anything) shows up.

xv.

🕵️ See the matrix: the Ironic Algorithm

One of Hudson's most quoted ideas. Whatever emotion you refuse to feel, you summon into your life — in precisely the shape you were avoiding.

Whatever emotion you're trying to avoid, you are inviting into your life in exactly the way that you're trying to avoid it.

Joe Hudson

His three-step way to spot it for yourself:

  1. Name an unwanted emotion

    Pick a feeling you spend energy avoiding — anger, failure, loneliness, being out of control.

  2. List how you avoid it

    Write the moves: suppress, appease, overwork, leave, control, go quiet.

  3. Notice you're creating it

    See that each avoidance manufactures the very feeling. The trap becomes visible — and visible, it loosens.

😶
Avoid feeling angry → suppress it → become passive-aggressive → more anger.
😰
Avoid feeling like a failure → don't try → fail anyway.
🧳
Avoid feeling lonely → keep moving, stay busy → feel lonely in the crowd.
🎛️
Avoid feeling out of control → grip and flail → spiral out of control.
xvi.

🌿 The simplest tool: "…and that's okay"

From Hudson's course, an exercise almost embarrassingly simple — and, students report, one of the most powerful. You name what you feel and add three words that dissolve the inner argument.

I feel angry — and that's okay.

The "and that's okay" isn't approval of behaviour — it's permission to feel. It removes the second-order suffering: the anger about the anger, the shame about the need. Hudson's partnered version has someone reflect it back — "You feel angry, and that's okay" — because being witnessed without judgement is what most of us never got.

xvii.

🗯️ The inner critic is always wrong

The self-attack that anger turns into has its own Hudson teaching. His claim is bracing:

There's nothing the critical voice in your head says to you that is true.

Joe Hudson

Two of his moves with it. First, don't fight it — relate to it. Treat the critic like a scared child: "I see you're scared, but I've got this." Defensiveness and argument only feed it; understanding disarms it. Second, the desert-island test:

🏝️ The thought experiment: imagine you're stranded alone on an island for a decade — fed, safe, no one to perform for. Who walks out? Hudson's point: you'd become the person you're trying to become without any of the self-criticism — which proves the criticism was never what got you there. "You're not going to get where you want to go by being a shitty boss to yourself."

This is the same insight as the anger work, pointed inward. Self-criticism is often anger with nowhere safe to go. Give the anger a clean outward channel and the inner critic loses its fuel.

xviii.

⛽ Clean fuel vs. dirty fuel

You can drive achievement with anger, fear, and self-criticism — Hudson calls that dirty fuel. It works, but it corrodes the engine. The cleaner, more sustainable fuel is enjoyment.

🛢️Dirty fuel

Stress, fear, anger, the lash of the inner critic. Gets results, drains you, and makes the whole system fragile and tense.

⚡Clean fuel

Enjoyment, wonder, care. You finish with more energy than you started — Hudson's actual definition of efficiency.

If you figure out how to enjoy what you do 10% more, and you succeed, you are 10% more efficient.

Joe Hudson

The link to anger: anger is dirty fuel when it runs you, clean information when you feel it cleanly. The aim isn't to never feel it — it's to stop running your life on it, and on its inward cousin, self-criticism.

xix.

🔑 Three quick reframes

⚖️
A "should" is two competing desires.

"I should get up" = I want to stay in bed and I want to be up. Name both honestly and the paralysis eases. Anger often hides a buried "should" — two wants at war.

❓
Ask "how" or "what", not "why".

"Why did you do that?" accuses. "How did that benefit you?" / "What's true here?" opens. Use it on others — and on yourself, instead of the interrogation.

🫁
Keep 10% of attention in the body.

One inch below the navel, even mid-conversation. It's how you feel the gut clench in real time — the early-warning system for anger before it becomes a story.

The Brain

The neuroscience of why feeling-and-naming actually works.

xx.

🧠 What's happening in your brain

Hudson's "feel it, name it, don't resist it" isn't just poetic — it maps onto how the brain actually regulates anger. Two players do most of the work:

🚨
The alarm

Amygdala

Your threat detector. It fires first and fast when a boundary is crossed — heat, heartbeat, the urge to strike or flee. It doesn't do nuance; it does danger, now.

🧭
The regulator

Prefrontal cortex

The reasoning, choosing part — the brain's "CEO." It can keep the alarm in proportion, but under stress its line to the amygdala weakens, and the alarm takes the wheel.

⚡
The "amygdala hijack." When the alarm overwhelms the regulator, you react before you choose — the lash-out, the shutdown. This is the kink in the hose, seen from the inside.

Here's the part that turns Hudson's advice into neuroscience. In Matthew Lieberman's UCLA studies, simply putting a feeling into words — "this is anger" — measurably calmed the amygdala and lit up the regulating prefrontal cortex. Naming doesn't suppress the emotion; it brings the thinking brain back online.

🔥Feel italarm fires
→
🏷️Name it"I'm angry"
→
🧠Brain shiftsregulator re-engages
→
🧘Chooseclean response
💡 Why this is hopeful for you: the amygdala–prefrontal link strengthens with practice — affect labeling and mindfulness measurably improve it over time. "Using anger well" is a trainable circuit, not a fixed temperament. Every time you feel-and-name instead of suppress-or-explode, you're laying down the wiring.

xxiii.

🌱 The practice

  1. Welcome it

    Not "I shouldn't be angry," but a turn toward it. The emotion you most resist is the one carrying the information — a rejected part of you asking to be felt.

  2. Drop into the body

    Where is it? Heat in the chest, the jaw, the hands? Stay with the sensation, not the narration. Let it move instead of managing it.

  3. Ask the three questions

    What am I fighting for? What boundary was crossed? What clarity am I after?

  4. Wait for the shift

    Let it run until the relief and the clear next step arrive — not more fight.

  5. Speak it clean, or act

    Own it — "I'm angry because…" — make the request, set the boundary. The goal was never to stop feeling anger. It's to never need to attack anyone with it.

xxiv.

🎬 Worked scenarios

The same anger, run through the kink and run clean. Tap a situation to see both paths and the three questions applied.

Kink

"You always do this. I knew I couldn't count on you." → they get defensive, you feel guilty for snapping, nothing's resolved.

Clean

Feel the heat, name it. "I'm angry — I'd been looking forward to this, and I need to feel I can rely on our plans."

What do I care about? Reliability; feeling chosen. Boundary crossed? My time and anticipation dismissed. Clarity sought? A real conversation about whether plans hold.
Kink

Say nothing, smile, then stew for days and snipe in the next meeting. The anger leaks sideways and you look like the difficult one.

Clean

"I'm frustrated — that was my analysis, and I want my contribution named. Can we set that straight?"

What do I care about? Fairness; being seen. Boundary crossed? Authorship of my work. Clarity sought? Visible credit, going forward.
Kink

Turn it on yourself — "I must have done something wrong" — spiral into anxious self-blame, then go cold to protect yourself.

Clean

Notice the anger under the anxiety. "I felt hurt and a bit angry when I didn't hear from you — I missed you and didn't know what was going on."

What do I care about? The connection; mattering to them. Boundary crossed? Being left in the dark. Clarity sought? What happened — and that I'm allowed to ask.

Notice the third one: the anger arrives dressed as anxiety and self-blame. Finding the anger underneath is the move — it points back outward, toward connection, instead of inward, toward self-attack.
