The paper

What you'll be asked to do

The Cambridge B2 First Writing paper takes 80 minutes and is split into two parts. You must complete both. Each part is worth 20 marks (40 total) and the two carry equal weight in your final Writing score.

Part 1 — Compulsory

Opinion essay

You're given a topic and two ideas. You must write an essay explaining which idea is more important, and add a third idea of your own.

  • Length: 140–190 words
  • Register: semi-formal / neutral
  • Focus: opinion with reasons and examples
Part 2 — Choose one

Pick from four task types

You're given three options from the four below. Each has its own context, reader and register.

All Part 2 tasks: 140–190 words.

Strategy

How to manage the 80 minutes

  • Part 1 — 40 min: 5 planning, 25 writing, 10 checking.
  • Part 2 — 40 min: 5 choosing and planning, 25 writing, 10 checking.
  • Always plan before you write — examiners reward clear organisation, and unplanned essays drift.
  • Word count matters: under-length loses Content marks; massively over-length suggests poor control.
Part 1 — 140–190 words

The opinion essay

You'll see a topic (often introduced as something discussed in class) and two ideas related to the topic. Your task is to discuss both ideas, say which is more important, and add a third idea of your own.

Semi-formal Neutral

What examiners want to see

  • Both given ideas discussed — don't ignore either one
  • A third idea of your own that's clearly different from the two given
  • A clear opinion on which idea is more important, with reasons
  • Organised paragraphs with basic linking
  • A range of B2-level vocabulary and grammar

Suggested structure

  1. Introduction (20–30 words): introduce the topic and signal that you'll discuss multiple points.
  2. Idea 1 (40–50 words): discuss the first given idea with a reason or example.
  3. Idea 2 (40–50 words): discuss the second given idea. Say which you think is more important and why.
  4. Your own idea (30–40 words): add your third point with brief support.
  5. Conclusion (20–30 words): restate your opinion clearly.

Four or five short paragraphs is the safest structure at B2.

Worked example

Sample task

Topic: In your English class, you have been discussing ways to protect the environment. Now your teacher has asked you to write an essay.

Write an essay using all the notes and giving reasons for your point of view.

"The most important thing people can do to help the environment is to change their daily habits."

Notes — Write about:

  1. recycling
  2. using public transport
  3. ……………… (your own idea)
Model answer — band 5

Many people believe that changing everyday habits is the best way to protect the environment. There are several things we can all do, and in this essay I will discuss three of them.

Firstly, recycling is something everyone can do at home. By sorting waste and reusing materials, we reduce the amount of rubbish that ends up in landfills. However, recycling alone is not enough to solve the problem.

In my opinion, using public transport is even more important. Cars are one of the biggest sources of pollution, and if more people took buses or trains, we could significantly reduce carbon emissions. This is particularly true in large cities, where traffic is already a serious issue.

Another point worth considering is reducing meat consumption. The meat industry produces enormous amounts of greenhouse gases, and eating less meat is one of the simplest changes an individual can make.

In conclusion, while recycling and public transport are both valuable, I believe that changing what we eat may have the greatest overall impact on the environment.

Word count: 175. Semi-formal register, five-paragraph structure, both notes covered plus own idea, clear opinion with reasons.

Part 2 — 140–190 words

Choose one task from three

The exam offers three of the four task types below. Read all three carefully before choosing — pick the one whose context, reader and register you can handle most confidently, not just the one with the most familiar topic.

Task type

Article

Engaging Semi-formal

Articles are written for a magazine, website or school publication. They should engage the reader with a personal voice, rhetorical questions, and vivid examples. Don't be bland — examiners want to see personality.

What examiners want to see

  • An interesting title that catches attention
  • An opening that pulls the reader in — a question, an observation, a bold statement
  • A personal voice — first person is expected
  • Concrete examples, not just vague opinions
  • A closing thought that the reader takes away

Suggested structure

  1. Title: short and engaging — not just the topic restated.
  2. Introduction (30–40 words): hook the reader with a question or interesting statement.
  3. Development (80–110 words): two paragraphs developing your ideas with examples.
  4. Conclusion (20–30 words): a thought worth remembering — not a flat summary.
Worked example

Sample task

You see this announcement in an English-language magazine:

We'd like to hear from our readers! Write an article about a hobby or activity that has changed your life. Tell us what you do and why you would recommend it to others.

Write your article in 140–190 words.

Model answer — band 5

The Joy of Running

Have you ever tried something you were absolutely sure you would hate? That was me and running two years ago.

A friend persuaded me to join a beginners' running group, and I reluctantly agreed. The first few sessions were painful — my legs ached and I could barely finish a kilometre. But within a month, something changed. I started looking forward to it. Running became the part of my day when I could switch off from work, think clearly, and feel genuinely good about myself.

What I love most is how simple it is. You don't need expensive equipment or a gym membership — just a pair of trainers and the willingness to step outside. It has improved my fitness, my sleep, and, surprisingly, my confidence.

If you've never tried running, give it a chance. You might be surprised by how much it changes your day.

Word count: 156. Engaging title, direct reader address, personal anecdote with specific detail, recommendation with reasons.

Task type

Email / Letter

Formal Informal

At B2, you might be asked to write a formal letter or email (to a manager, an organisation) or an informal email (to a friend). Read the task carefully to identify which register is needed — the tone must match the reader.

What examiners want to see

  • The correct register — formal or informal as required
  • All content points from the task addressed
  • Appropriate opening and closing formulae
  • Clear paragraphing — one main point per paragraph
  • A clear purpose — complaining, inviting, requesting, thanking, etc.

Suggested structure

  1. Greeting: Dear Sir/Madam (formal) or Hi/Dear + name (informal)
  2. Opening (20–30 words): state why you're writing.
  3. Body (80–110 words): two or three paragraphs covering the content points.
  4. Closing (20–30 words): sign off appropriately.
Common register mistakes
  • Formal: avoid contractions (I'm, don't), slang, emojis
  • Informal: avoid overly stiff language (I am writing to inform you…); use contractions naturally
  • Mixing registers — starting formally and drifting into chatty language
Worked example — informal

Sample task

You have received this email from your English-speaking friend Alex:

I'm thinking about coming to visit you next month! What's the weather usually like at that time of year? Is there anything special we should do while I'm there? Also, is there anything you'd like me to bring from here?

Write your email in 140–190 words.

Model answer — band 5

Hi Alex,

Great news — I'd love to see you! Next month is actually one of the best times to visit. The weather should be warm and sunny, around 25 degrees most days, so pack light clothes and don't forget sunscreen.

There's loads we can do while you're here. I'd definitely recommend a day trip to the coast — the beaches are beautiful and there are some great seafood restaurants nearby. We could also visit the old town, which has some amazing architecture and a fantastic market on Saturdays.

As for things to bring, honestly you don't need to worry about anything. But if it's not too much trouble, could you bring some of that tea I liked when I visited you? I haven't been able to find it here!

Let me know your dates and I'll sort everything out.

Can't wait!

Sam

Word count: 161. Informal register, friendly tone, all three content points addressed, natural contractions and chatty style.

Task type

Report

Neutral Semi-formal

Reports are written for a teacher, principal, or committee. They describe a situation and make recommendations. Use headings to organise your report — this is what distinguishes it from an essay.

What examiners want to see

  • Clear headings — this is the defining feature of a report
  • An impersonal, neutral tone — avoid chatty language
  • Description of the current situation followed by recommendations
  • Recommendations that are specific and practical
  • Logical organisation under each heading

Suggested structure

  1. Introduction: state the purpose of the report in one or two sentences.
  2. Current situation (1–2 headings): describe what exists or what happened.
  3. Recommendations: suggest specific improvements or actions.
Worked example

Sample task

The director of your English language school has asked students to write a report on the school's social programme (trips, events, and clubs). You should describe what is currently available and suggest improvements.

Write your report in 140–190 words.

Model answer — band 5

Introduction

The purpose of this report is to evaluate the school's current social programme and suggest ways it could be improved.

Current provision

The school currently offers a weekly film night and a monthly trip to a local attraction. While both are popular, several students have mentioned that the range of activities is limited and that events are not well publicised.

Areas for improvement

A number of students have expressed interest in sports activities and cooking classes. In addition, it would be beneficial to create a noticeboard or social media page where upcoming events are announced in advance.

Recommendations

I would recommend introducing at least two new weekly activities, such as a football club and a cooking workshop. Furthermore, a dedicated Instagram page for the social programme would help increase student participation.

Word count: 148. Clear headings, impersonal tone, description followed by specific recommendations.

Task type

Review

Semi-formal Engaging

Reviews are written for a magazine, website or English teacher. You describe something (a film, book, restaurant, event), evaluate it (strengths and weaknesses), and give a recommendation. A good review has both positive and negative points.

What examiners want to see

  • A clear description of the thing being reviewed — the reader needs to understand what it is
  • Evaluation — not just "it was great" but why it was great (or not)
  • At least one reservation or weakness — pure praise is not convincing
  • A clear recommendation at the end — who should experience this?
  • An engaging tone — somewhere between formal and chatty

Suggested structure

  1. Introduction (25–35 words): name the thing, give basic context.
  2. Description (35–45 words): briefly describe what it is / what happens.
  3. Evaluation (50–70 words): what's good, what's less good, why.
  4. Recommendation (25–35 words): who would enjoy it, and would you recommend it?
Worked example

Sample task

You see this in an English-language magazine:

Reviews wanted! Have you seen a good film recently? Write a review of a film you have seen, including what you liked and didn't like about it, and whether you would recommend it.

Write your review in 140–190 words.

Model answer — band 5

I recently watched The Shawshank Redemption, a classic drama that I had somehow never seen before, and I can honestly say it deserves its reputation.

The film follows Andy Dufresne, a banker wrongly convicted of murder, and his friendship with a fellow prisoner over twenty years. The story moves slowly but deliberately, building towards one of the most satisfying endings I have ever seen.

What impressed me most was the acting. Tim Robbins and Morgan Freeman give performances that feel completely real, and the relationship between their characters is both moving and believable. The film also looks beautiful — every shot of the prison is carefully composed.

If I had one criticism, it would be the pacing. Some scenes in the middle feel a little slow, and younger viewers might find it old-fashioned compared to modern thrillers.

Overall, I would highly recommend this film to anyone who enjoys character-driven stories. It is a film that stays with you long after the credits roll.

Word count: 174. Description, balanced evaluation (praise + reservation), clear recommendation with target audience.

How marks are awarded

The four assessment criteria

Cambridge examiners score every Writing task on four equally weighted criteria, each on a 0–5 scale. That gives a maximum of 20 marks per task, and 40 marks for the paper. No single criterion can rescue you if another is weak — a linguistically impressive essay that ignores half the task still scores badly on Content.

Content

0–5 marks

Have you done what the task asked? All required points covered, nothing essential missing, the target reader would be informed or persuaded as appropriate.

5All content relevant; target reader fully informed.
3Minor irrelevances or omissions; reader on the whole informed.
1Significant irrelevance and omissions; reader minimally informed.

Communicative Achievement

0–5 marks

Is the register and tone right? A formal letter that drifts into slang, or an article with no engaging features, is penalised here regardless of how accurate the language is.

5Conventions of the task used effectively; holds target reader's attention and communicates ideas.
3Conventions used generally appropriately; straightforward ideas communicated.
1Conventions attempted but with limited success.

Organisation

0–5 marks

Is the text coherently structured? Examiners look at paragraphing, flow of ideas, and cohesive devices. Reward variety — not just "Firstly… Secondly… In conclusion".

5Well-organised and coherent; uses a variety of linking words and cohesive devices.
3Generally well-organised; uses basic linking words appropriately.
1Some organisation; limited use of cohesive devices.

Language

0–5 marks

How wide and accurate is your range? At B2, examiners want to see vocabulary beyond the basics and a mix of simple and complex grammar. Errors are accepted if they don't impede communication.

5Range of vocabulary and grammatical forms used with control and flexibility.
3Everyday vocabulary used appropriately; simple and some complex grammar with reasonable control.
1Basic vocabulary; simple grammatical forms; errors may impede communication.
Practical takeaways

What this means for your writing

  • Cover every content point. If the task gives you three things to write about, write about all three. Missing one caps your Content score.
  • Match the genre. A report needs headings. An article needs a title and personality. An informal email needs contractions and a friendly tone.
  • Vary your linkers. Using "Firstly… Secondly… Thirdly…" in every essay limits your Organisation score. Mix it up.
  • Show range. Don't just use "good" and "bad" — use "beneficial", "impressive", "disappointing". Show the examiner you have vocabulary.
  • Check your word count. 140–190 words. Too short means missing content; too long suggests you can't control your writing.
